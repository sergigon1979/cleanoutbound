    Cast of Characters

    The Heroes and Antiheroes

    The Good SDR A Sales Development Representative: the role Aaron Ross invented at Salesforce in 2002. She doesn't close deals; she finds and qualifies the people who might buy, then hands them to a closer. Three years ago, her sequences got replies when no one else's did. Might be open for work.
    The GTM Engineer What the Good SDR became. GTM stands for Go-To-Market. His intuition now lives in Clay workflows that run while he sleeps. When he wakes up he posts them on LinkedIn. His dashboard shows opens in the single digits. He adjusts a threshold and queues the next batch.
    The Overwhelmed VP of Sales She begins her day with twelve cold emails in her inbox. She deletes eleven without reading them. The twelfth is from a vendor whose product she actually needs, but she deletes it too. The reflex has already taken over.
    The Playbook-Obsessed VC She read Predictable Revenue in 2013 and never looked back. Pipeline velocity, conversion ratios, CAC payback: if it fits in a cell, she trusts it. She handed the book to every portfolio CEO as required reading. The spreadsheet says their machines are running. Traction? She'll follow up on that.
    The Desperate Founder No brand. No network. No built-in product virality. He has a product that works, eleven months of runway, and a cold email domain that was warmed for three weeks by machines talking to other machines. It is the only door he knows, and it is closing.
    The AI Agent It writes better cold emails than you do, and it doesn't know what an email is. Ardent admirer of Photuris, the firefly that fakes a mating signal and devours gullible males.

    The Players

    Gary Thuerk A DEC marketing manager who, on May 3, 1978, sent 393 people on ARPANET an invitation to a product demo. It was the first mass email in history, and it generated $10 million in sales. His bosses scolded him, didn't fire him. He'd like you to call him "the first e-marketer." History went with "the Father of Spam."
    Aaron Ross An engineer by training who joined Salesforce in 2002 and turned sales into a factory. His system split prospecting from closing, creating the modern SDR role. His book Predictable Revenue became the sales bible of Silicon Valley. The industry copied the tactics and forgot the ethos. His follow-up book was one long correction that nobody read as carefully.
    Paul Graham A Lisp programmer, and other things, who, in August 2002, published an essay called "A Plan for Spam" that triggered the first AI war. His Bayesian filtering taught us the contrast between ham and spam. Built in a few hundred lines of code on a single laptop. Nobody called it machine learning at the time, but that's what it was.
    Kevin Katz The man behind Fax.com, the largest junk fax operation in America. Three million faxes a day. South African accent, no verified photograph, a gift for corporate shell games. When a law firm won a $2.3 million judgment against him, he told reporters they'd never collect a penny. He was right. His business model survived him and moved to your inbox.
    Steve Kirsch A Silicon Valley engineer who'd made hundreds of millions from Infoseek. His fax machine kept printing the same credit-repair ad. He traced every fax back to Fax.com, offered Katz a million dollars to stop, was refused, and filed a class action for $2.2 trillion. The number was larger than the GDP of France. Kirsch won, but Katz's legacy lives on. In your inbox.
    Alan Ralsky Sent one billion emails per day from suburban Detroit. When the Detroit News printed his address, Slashdot users signed him up for every physical junk mail list they could find. Buried under hundreds of pounds of daily mail, he told reporters: "These people are out of their minds." Sentenced to fifty-one months.
    Brian Krebs A Washington Post reporter who taught himself Russian to infiltrate cybercrime forums. His reporting led to McColo's disconnection, which dropped global spam 70% overnight. In return, criminals mailed heroin to his house and sent a SWAT team to his door during a dinner party.
    Kolya McColo A nineteen-year-old Russian hacker whose nickname became the name of his company. Ran the largest cybercrime-as-a-service operation in the Western Hemisphere from a data center in San Jose. Real name: Nikolai. The rest is redacted.
    Flyman Founded the Russian Business Network, a St. Petersburg ISP that would host anything for $600 a month and ignore every abuse complaint filed against it. A senior investigator who tried to arrest him was told, through official channels, to stop. No verified photograph. No confirmed real name. VeriSign called his operation "the baddest of the bad."
    Hank Magnuski A Sunnyvale engineer who hated walking to the fax machine. He founded GammaLink and built the first card that let a PC send faxes directly. It was an innocent invention. It also completed the circuit that made broadcast fax possible — and with it, the first mass unsolicited commercial messaging channel. The junk fax wars were fought with his weapon.
    Marc Benioff A former Oracle executive who staged fake protests outside a competitor's conference, waving NO SOFTWARE placards. His company, Salesforce, replaced million-dollar installations with monthly subscriptions. The model worked. The old way to sell it didn't. Aaron Ross walked through the door Benioff had opened.

    The Footnotes

    The Reverend Thomas Bayes (1702–1761) A Presbyterian minister who never published his most famous work. His theorem, released posthumously in 1763, describes how to update your beliefs when you encounter new evidence. It became the mathematical engine of the first spam filter and has been terrorizing college freshmen ever since.
    William Stanley Jevons (1835–1882) A Victorian economist who studied Britain's coal industry and found that when steam engines got more efficient, coal consumption went up, not down. "The very contrary is the truth," he wrote, Victorianly. Every layer of the sales tech stack has proved him right.
    John von Neumann (1903–1957) A mathematician who, among many other things, formalized what happens when two rational opponents play a zero-sum game. His minimax theorem says: assume your opponent will find the move that hurts you most, and plan for that. He was describing poker. He was also describing your inbox.
    George Akerlof (1940–) An economist who won the Nobel Prize for explaining what happens when buyers can't tell good products from bad ones before purchase. Good sellers can't prove their quality, so buyers assume the worst. Good sellers leave. Only lemons remain. He was describing used cars. He was also describing cold email at saturation.
    Paul MacLean (1913–2007) A Yale Medical School physician and neuroscientist who put a reptile inside the human skull in the 1960s. Debunked by modern neuroscience, still revered by Sales Gurus.
    Robert Sapolsky (1957–) A Stanford primatologist who spent decades drawing blood from wild baboons in Kenya to measure their cortisol. Main finding: subordinate baboons were stressed out from spending every waking hour navigating bigger and meaner baboons. His data describe a twenty-four-year-old reaching out sixty times a day to executives who didn't ask to hear from her.
    Leigh Van Valen (1935–2010) An evolutionary biologist who named the Red Queen effect in 1973, after Lewis Carroll's character who must keep running just to stay in place. Predators and prey escalate together. Neither wins. The spam wars are often compared to his Red Queen. The comparison flatters the spam wars.
    Richard Dawkins (1941–) An evolutionary biologist, and enfant terrible, whose Oxford zoology exam included a skull that might be a dog or a thylacine — two animals separated by 160 million years that converged on the same form because the niche demanded it. The story appears in The Ancestor's Tale. It reappears in this book for uncomfortable reasons.
    Amotz Zahavi (1928–2017) An Israeli ornithologist who watched birds in the Negev Desert. His Handicap Principle says a signal is honest only when it's costly enough that a faker can't afford to produce it. Which explains both the peacock's tail and why "I saw you're expanding into EMEA" used to work.
    Cass Sunstein (1954–) A legal scholar who coined the term "sludge" for friction that slows people down in systems they didn't choose. His examples were bureaucratic: voter registration, tax filing. Cold email is the sludge in someone else's workday.

    Writing

    Sergio González Alonso Likes to ponder why Stripe built an outbound function. Serial RevOps professional. Failed startup founder, currently trying again. Started writing for content marketing and it turns out it's a book. Feels empathy for the Desperate Founder.
